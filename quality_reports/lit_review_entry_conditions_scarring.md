## 1. Directly Related Papers

These papers address the same or closely overlapping question: how labor market entry conditions affect workers' working conditions, skills, or job quality over their careers.

---

### Arellano-Bover (2022) -- The Effect of Labor Market Conditions at Entry on Workers' Long-Term Skills

- **Journal:** Review of Economics and Statistics, 104(5), 1028--1045
- **Proximity:** 1 (directly competes)
- **Main contribution:** First paper to show that labor market conditions during the education-to-work transition affect workers' long-term cognitive skills, using cross-country data from PIAAC (19 countries). Proposes firm-level skill development heterogeneity as mechanism.
- **Identification strategy:** Cohort variation in unemployment rates at ages 18--25, controlling for country and cohort fixed effects.
- **Key finding:** Workers who faced higher unemployment at ages 18--25 have significantly lower cognitive skills at ages 36--59. Effects are larger for those with less-educated parents. Unemployment rates at ages 26--35 have no effect.
- **Data:** PIAAC (adult skills survey) + German SOEP panel for mechanism.
- **Relevance:** This is the closest competitor. Our paper must clearly differentiate: we use EWCS (broader working conditions, not just cognitive skills), focus on multiple dimensions of job quality (autonomy, intensity, physical environment, social environment), and potentially different identification approach or additional waves of data.

---

### Arellano-Bover (2024) -- Career Consequences of Firm Heterogeneity for Young Workers: First Job and Firm Size

- **Journal:** Journal of Labor Economics, 42(2), 549--589
- **Proximity:** 2 (closely related, different angle)
- **Main contribution:** Shows that starting at a larger firm persistently improves long-term labor market outcomes, using Spanish administrative data. Develops IV approach that holds business cycle fixed while leveraging variation in labor demand composition.
- **Identification strategy:** Instrumental variables exploiting within-cohort variation in firm-size composition of labor demand, holding cycle conditions fixed.
- **Key finding:** Initially matching with a larger firm persistently improves earnings and other outcomes through subsequent jobs. Mechanism is better skill development at large firms.
- **Data:** Spanish Social Security administrative records.
- **Relevance:** Establishes the firm quality mechanism for why entry conditions matter -- recession entrants start at worse firms, which stunts skill development. Complements our working conditions focus.

---

### Mahajan, Patki, and Stuber (forthcoming) -- Bad Times, Bad Jobs? How Recessions Affect Early Career Trajectories

- **Journal:** Journal of Labor Economics (forthcoming, IZA DP 16898)
- **Proximity:** 1 (directly competes)
- **Main contribution:** Studies how labor market entry during recessions affects early career trajectories, finding that one-quarter of recession-induced earnings losses are compensated by non-pay amenities. Uses a revealed-preference estimator of employer quality.
- **Identification strategy:** Cohort variation in business cycle conditions at entry, revealed-preference estimator for non-pay amenities.
- **Key finding:** Labor market entry during recessions generates a 6% reduction in cumulated earnings over the first 15 years. However, 25% of these losses are offset by non-pay amenities (better working conditions at lower-paying jobs), suggesting pure earnings estimates overstate welfare costs.
- **Data:** US administrative data (LEHD).
- **Relevance:** MAJOR SCOOPING RISK. This paper directly addresses working conditions as compensating differentials for recession-entry earnings losses. Our paper must clearly differentiate: (1) European context, (2) direct measurement of working conditions via EWCS rather than revealed-preference, (3) multiple dimensions of working conditions.

---

### Arellano-Bover and Saltiel (2024) -- Differences in On-the-Job Learning across Firms

- **Journal:** Journal of Labor Economics (forthcoming, CESifo WP 11031)
- **Proximity:** 3 (closely related mechanism -- CORRECTED from 2; this is about firm-level learning heterogeneity, not entry conditions directly)
- **Main contribution:** Documents large disparities across firms in on-the-job learning opportunities using admin data from Brazil and Italy. Uses clustering methodology to categorize firms into discrete "learning classes."
- **Identification strategy:** Firm-level clustering of wage growth distributions, validated with movers/stayers designs.
- **Key finding:** Mincerian returns to experience vary widely across firm classes. Multiple tests point to portable, general human capital interpretation.
- **Data:** Administrative employer-employee data from Brazil and Italy.
- **Relevance:** Establishes the theoretical mechanism: firms differ in learning environments, which explains why initial firm quality matters for long-term skills and working conditions.

## 2. Same Question, Different Method or Setting

Papers studying entry conditions and career outcomes in different settings or with different methods.

---

### Schwandt and von Wachter (2019) -- Unlucky Cohorts: Estimating the Long-Term Effects of Entering the Labor Market in a Recession in Large Cross-Sectional Data Sets

- **Journal:** Journal of Labor Economics, 37(S1), S161--S198
- **Proximity:** 3 (closely related methodology, but about earnings not working conditions -- CORRECTED from 2)
- **Main contribution:** Develops methodology for estimating long-term scarring effects using large repeated cross-sections (CPS/ACS). Shows persistent effects on earnings, wages, and government transfers, with stronger effects for less advantaged groups.
- **Identification strategy:** Cohort x state variation in unemployment rates at labor market entry, estimated from repeated cross-sections with cohort, state, and year fixed effects.
- **Key finding:** A 1 pp increase in state UR at entry reduces earnings by 1.8% at entry, fading to near zero after 10--15 years. Less educated workers face larger and more persistent losses. High school graduates and dropouts suffer stronger income losses than college graduates.
- **Data:** US Census/ACS/CPS (1976--2015).
- **Relevance:** Gold standard methodology for cross-sectional scarring estimation. Our paper follows similar cross-sectional approach with EWCS. Key methodological precedent.

---

### Oreopoulos, von Wachter, and Heisz (2012) -- The Short- and Long-Term Career Effects of Graduating in a Recession

- **Journal:** American Economic Journal: Applied Economics, 4(1), 1--29
- **Proximity:** 3 (related, different outcome and setting)
- **Main contribution:** Seminal paper using Canadian administrative employer-employee data to estimate earnings losses from graduating in a recession. Shows recovery occurs through job mobility toward better firms.
- **Identification strategy:** Cohort variation in provincial unemployment rates at graduation, exploiting mandatory graduation timing from Canadian universities.
- **Key finding:** Graduating in a recession causes earnings losses of ~9% initially, halving within 5 years and disappearing after ~10 years. Less advantaged graduates suffer more persistent effects. Recovery occurs through switching to better-paying firms.
- **Data:** Canadian university-employer-employee matched administrative data.
- **Relevance:** Foundational paper establishing firm quality mechanism: recession graduates start at worse firms, then partially recover through mobility.

---

### Kahn (2010) -- The Long-Term Labor Market Consequences of Graduating from College in a Bad Economy

- **Journal:** Labour Economics, 17(2), 303--316
- **Proximity:** 3 (related, wages focus)
- **Main contribution:** Early evidence on persistent wage effects from entry conditions using NLSY79. Shows that graduates entering worse economies end up in lower-level occupations.
- **Identification strategy:** National and state unemployment rates at time of college graduation as exogenous variation.
- **Key finding:** Large, negative wage effects that persist for the entire 20-year study period. Graduates in worse economies sort into lower-level occupations.
- **Data:** NLSY79 (white males, college graduates 1979--1989).
- **Relevance:** Among the first papers to document persistent occupational downgrading as a scarring mechanism -- directly relevant to working conditions through occupation channel.

---

### Altonji, Kahn, and Speer (2016) -- Cashier or Consultant? Entry Labor Market Conditions, Field of Study, and Career Success

- **Journal:** Journal of Labor Economics, 34(S1), S361--S401
- **Proximity:** 3 (related, occupation/skill mismatch)
- **Main contribution:** Studies how entry conditions interact with college major to determine initial occupation placement and earnings. Shows wider earnings gaps across majors in recessions.
- **Identification strategy:** Cohort variation in national unemployment rates at graduation, interacted with field of study.
- **Key finding:** A large recession reduces initial earnings by ~10%. Those in typically higher-earning majors suffer smaller declines. Cyclical sensitivity of college graduate labor demand increased over time.
- **Data:** ACS (US college graduates, classes of 1974--2011).
- **Relevance:** Demonstrates occupational downgrading mechanism -- recession graduates take jobs below their skill level, relevant to working conditions through mismatch.

---

### Cockx and Ghirelli (2016) -- Scars of Recessions in a Rigid Labor Market

- **Journal:** Labour Economics, 41, 162--176
- **Proximity:** 2 (closely related, European rigid market)
- **Main contribution:** Studies scarring in Belgium (Flanders), a rigid labor market. Shows differential channels by education: wages for high-educated, working hours for low-educated.
- **Identification strategy:** Cohort variation in provincial unemployment rates at graduation, exploiting educational system structure in Flanders.
- **Key finding:** For low-educated men, typical recession reduces working time and earnings by ~4.5% up to 12 years after graduation, with little wage effect. For high-educated, hourly wage penalty increases with experience, reaching ~-6% after 10 years.
- **Data:** Belgian administrative data (Crossroads Bank for Social Security).
- **Relevance:** Key European evidence showing that in rigid labor markets, scarring operates through different channels (hours vs. wages), which is directly relevant to working conditions outcomes.

---

### Fernandez-Kranz and Rodriguez-Planas (2018) -- The Perfect Storm: Graduating during a Recession in a Segmented Labor Market

- **Journal:** ILR Review, 71(2), 492--524
- **Proximity:** 2 (closely related, dual labor market)
- **Main contribution:** Studies entry conditions effects in Spain's dual labor market with temporary vs. permanent contracts. Shows different scarring channels by education.
- **Identification strategy:** Cohort variation in regional unemployment rates at entry, using Spanish administrative data.
- **Key finding:** Non-college workers: persistent earnings losses driven by lower employment probability. College graduates: losses driven by non-employment and fixed-term contract employment. Negative shock increases mobility but no earnings recovery -- just churning through secondary labor market jobs.
- **Data:** Spanish Social Security + Labor Force Survey.
- **Relevance:** Shows how labor market segmentation (temporary contracts) mediates entry condition effects in a key European setting.

---

### Liu, Salvanes, and Sorensen (2016) -- Good Skills in Bad Times: Cyclical Skill Mismatch and the Long-Term Effects of Graduating in a Recession

- **Journal:** European Economic Review, 84, 3--17
- **Proximity:** 2 (closely related, skill mismatch mechanism)
- **Main contribution:** Shows cyclical skill mismatch is a key mechanism behind persistent earnings losses from recession entry. First-job match quality explains up to half of short-term and most of long-term losses.
- **Identification strategy:** Cohort variation in national unemployment rates at college graduation in Norway.
- **Key finding:** Strong countercyclical pattern of skill mismatch among college graduates. Initial match quality may explain up to 50% of short-term and most of long-term earnings loss.
- **Data:** Norwegian administrative data.
- **Relevance:** Directly relevant mechanism: skill mismatch from bad entry conditions leads to worse working conditions (working below one's skill level).

---

### Genda, Kondo, and Ohta (2010) -- Long-Term Effects of a Recession at Labor Market Entry in Japan and the United States

- **Journal:** Journal of Human Resources, 45(1), 157--196
- **Proximity:** 3 (related, institutional comparison)
- **Main contribution:** Cross-country comparison of recession entry effects between Japan and US, emphasizing institutional mechanisms (school-based hiring, dismissal regulation).
- **Identification strategy:** Cohort variation in national unemployment rates at graduation, parallel analysis in two countries.
- **Key finding:** Persistent negative effects for less-educated Japanese men; only temporary effects for less-educated American men. Japanese school-based hiring and dismissal regulation prolong the loss.
- **Data:** Japanese Employment Status Survey + US CPS.
- **Relevance:** Institutional mediation of scarring effects -- relevant to our European cross-country analysis where institutions vary widely.

---

### Brunner and Kuhn (2014) -- The Impact of Labor Market Entry Conditions on Initial Job Assignment and Wages

- **Journal:** Journal of Population Economics, 27(3), 705--738
- **Proximity:** 3 (related, European evidence)
- **Main contribution:** Studies scarring in Austria, distinguishing blue-collar vs. white-collar workers. Finds permanent effects for blue-collar but temporary for white-collar.
- **Identification strategy:** Cohort variation in regional unemployment rates at entry, using Austrian Social Security data.
- **Key finding:** 1 pp increase in initial UR reduces initial wages by ~0.9%, with lifetime loss of ~1.3%. Effects are highly persistent/permanent for blue-collar workers; temporary (5--10 years) for white-collar.
- **Data:** Austrian Social Security administrative records.
- **Relevance:** European evidence showing heterogeneous persistence by worker type, relevant to our working conditions analysis where blue-collar workers likely face different conditions.

---

### Schmillen and Umkehrer (2019) -- Heterogeneous Effects of Entering the Labor Market During a Recession: New Evidence from Germany

- **Journal:** CESifo Economic Studies, 65(2), 177--203
- **Proximity:** 3 (related, German evidence)
- **Main contribution:** Studies entry effects in Germany using linked employer-employee data, exploiting institutional variation in graduation timing.
- **Identification strategy:** Institutional variation in graduation timing + regional variation in unemployment rates.
- **Key finding:** Less advantaged graduates experience persistent earnings declines, driven by reduced employment stability in sectors with higher collective bargaining coverage.
- **Data:** German IAB linked employer-employee data.
- **Relevance:** German institutional context with collective bargaining -- relevant to European institutional variation in our paper.

## 3. Core Scarring Literature (Different Outcomes)

Papers on scarring effects focusing on outcomes other than working conditions (wages, health, mortality, family).

---

### von Wachter (2020) -- The Persistent Effects of Initial Labor Market Conditions for Young Adults and Their Sources

- **Journal:** Journal of Economic Perspectives, 34(4), 168--194
- **Proximity:** 3 (survey, background)
- **Main contribution:** Comprehensive survey of the scarring literature. Synthesizes evidence on earnings, labor supply, occupation, employer quality, health, mortality, family formation, and criminal behavior.
- **Identification strategy:** Literature review.
- **Key finding:** Large initial effects on earnings that fade after 10--15 years, accompanied by occupation changes, job mobility, and employer characteristics changes. Also persistent effects on fertility, marriage, health, and mortality.
- **Data:** Survey of existing literature.
- **Relevance:** Essential framing paper. Identifies working conditions as an under-explored outcome relative to earnings and health.

---

### Schwandt and von Wachter (2020) -- Socioeconomic Decline and Death: Midlife Impacts of Graduating in a Recession

- **Journal:** NBER Working Paper 26638 (still unpublished as of March 2026)
- **Proximity:** 3 (related, health/mortality outcomes)
- **Main contribution:** Extends scarring analysis to mortality by cause, showing that recession entry cohorts suffer elevated mortality in middle age from behavior-related causes.
- **Identification strategy:** Same as Schwandt and von Wachter (2019) applied to mortality outcomes.
- **Key finding:** By age 50, one extra death per 10,000 for each pp increase in UR at graduation (~6% increase in age-specific mortality). Driven by heart disease, lung cancer, liver disease, and drug overdoses.
- **Data:** US Census/Vital Statistics (1976--2015).
- **Relevance:** Demonstrates that scarring extends to health and mortality -- working conditions may be a mechanism (stress, physical hazards, etc.).

---

### Rothstein (2023) -- The Lost Generation? Labor Market Outcomes for Post Great Recession Entrants

- **Journal:** Journal of Human Resources, 58(5), 1452--1479
- **Proximity:** 3 (related, structural break)
- **Main contribution:** Examines whether the Great Recession created unique scarring or whether there was a structural break in employment for college graduates.
- **Identification strategy:** Cohort analysis comparing pre- and post-Great Recession labor market entrants.
- **Key finding:** Adverse early conditions permanently reduce employment probabilities, with medium-term scarring on wages fading by early 30s. Identifies a dramatic structural break in employment rates beginning around the 2005 entry cohort.
- **Data:** US CPS (1995--2019).
- **Relevance:** Raises the question of whether scarring patterns are changing over time -- relevant for European context where 2008 crisis effects differed across countries.

---

### Huckfeldt (2022) -- Understanding the Scarring Effect of Recessions

- **Journal:** American Economic Review, 112(4), 1273--1310
- **Proximity:** 3 (related, structural model of scarring)
- **Main contribution:** Develops structural model explaining earnings cost of job loss through occupation displacement. Hiring is endogenously more selective in recessions, pushing workers to search in lower-skill occupations.
- **Identification strategy:** Structural DMP search-and-matching model with task-specific human capital.
- **Key finding:** Earnings cost of job loss is concentrated among those who find reemployment in lower-skill occupations. Cost and incidence of occupation displacement is higher for workers displaced during recessions.
- **Data:** SIPP (1996--2013).
- **Relevance:** Provides theoretical micro-foundation: recession entry forces workers into lower-skill jobs where working conditions may systematically differ.

---

### Hershbein and Kahn (2018) -- Do Recessions Accelerate Routine-Biased Technological Change? Evidence from Vacancy Postings

- **Journal:** American Economic Review, 108(7), 1737--1772
- **Proximity:** 3 (related, skill restructuring in recessions)
- **Main contribution:** Shows recessions accelerate the shift toward higher skill requirements, permanently altering job quality composition in affected areas.
- **Identification strategy:** Cross-MSA variation in Great Recession severity, using Burning Glass vacancy postings.
- **Key finding:** Skill requirements in job postings differentially increased in harder-hit MSAs. Effects persist through 2015, concentrated in routine-cognitive occupations.
- **Data:** Burning Glass vacancy data (2007--2015).
- **Relevance:** Shows that recessions permanently change the task/skill composition of available jobs, which directly affects working conditions for entrants.

---

### Oyer (2006) -- Initial Labor Market Conditions and Long-Term Outcomes for Economists

- **Journal:** Journal of Economic Perspectives, 20(3), 143--160
- **Proximity:** 4 (background, initial placement mechanism)
- **Main contribution:** Shows that initial job placement has a causal effect on long-term job characteristics for PhD economists. Better initial placement increases research productivity.
- **Identification strategy:** Macroeconomic conditions at graduation as instrument for initial placement.
- **Key finding:** Quality and type of initial job have causal effects on long-term outcomes. Better initial placement increases research productivity.
- **Data:** PhD economists from seven programs.
- **Relevance:** Demonstrates initial placement mechanism in a specific labor market, supporting the general argument that entry conditions shape career trajectories.

---

### Oyer (2008) -- The Making of an Investment Banker: Stock Market Shocks, Career Choice, and Lifetime Income

- **Journal:** Journal of Finance, 63(6), 2601--2628
- **Proximity:** 4 (background, initial placement mechanism)
- **Main contribution:** Shows that stock market conditions during MBA schooling causally determine Wall Street placement and long-term earnings.
- **Identification strategy:** Stock market conditions at MBA graduation as instrument for initial Wall Street placement.
- **Key finding:** Strong causal effect of initial placement on likelihood of working on Wall Street 3--20 years later, with substantial earnings differences.
- **Data:** Stanford MBA graduates.
- **Relevance:** Clean case study of how initial conditions lock in career paths, extending the mechanism to high-skill finance settings.

## 4. Working Conditions and Job Quality Literature

Papers on measurement, determinants, and consequences of working conditions.

---

### Eurofound (2017) -- Sixth European Working Conditions Survey: Overview Report

- **Journal:** Eurofound Report (Luxembourg: Publications Office of the European Union)
- **Proximity:** 3 (data documentation, background)
- **Main contribution:** Comprehensive analysis of working conditions across 35 European countries using 7 job quality indices: physical environment, work intensity, working time quality, social environment, skills and discretion, prospects, and earnings.
- **Identification strategy:** Descriptive analysis of survey data.
- **Key finding:** Job quality varies substantially across countries, sectors, and demographic groups. Five distinct job quality profiles identified. Overall improvement in some dimensions but persistent inequalities.
- **Data:** 6th EWCS (2015), 43,850 workers in 35 countries.
- **Relevance:** Primary data documentation for our project. Defines the job quality indices we will use as outcome variables.

---

### Green and Mostafa (2012) -- Trends in Job Quality in Europe

- **Journal:** Eurofound Report (5th EWCS analysis)
- **Proximity:** 3 (data/measurement, background)
- **Main contribution:** Develops four indices for measuring job quality: Earnings, Prospects, Intrinsic Job Quality, and Working Time Quality. Documents trends using EWCS data.
- **Identification strategy:** Descriptive trend analysis.
- **Key finding:** Non-wage aspects of job quality show mixed trends across Europe. Significant variation across countries in levels and trends.
- **Data:** 5th EWCS (2010).
- **Relevance:** Establishes the measurement framework for job quality indices that we will use or adapt.

---

### Green (2006) -- Demanding Work: The Paradox of Job Quality in the Affluent Economy

- **Journal:** Princeton University Press (book)
- **Proximity:** 4 (background on working conditions)
- **Main contribution:** Documents the paradox that despite rising pay and skill requirements, work intensification has increased while autonomy and job satisfaction have declined in affluent economies.
- **Identification strategy:** Descriptive, using survey data from multiple countries.
- **Key finding:** Substantial work intensification between 1992 and 2001, with effort remaining on a plateau between 2001 and 2006. Skills have risen but so have demands.
- **Data:** Multiple national and European working conditions surveys.
- **Relevance:** Background on the key outcome: what drives working conditions trends and why they matter for welfare beyond wages.

---

### Karasek (1979) -- Job Demands, Job Decision Latitude, and Mental Strain: Implications for Job Redesign

- **Journal:** Administrative Science Quarterly, 24, 285--308
- **Proximity:** 4 (theoretical background)
- **Main contribution:** Foundational model of job strain: high demands + low control = mental strain. The demand-control model remains the dominant framework for understanding working conditions and health.
- **Identification strategy:** Cross-sectional survey analysis (US and Sweden).
- **Key finding:** High job demands combined with low decision latitude are associated with mental strain and job dissatisfaction. High demands + high control leads to active learning.
- **Data:** US and Swedish national survey data.
- **Relevance:** Theoretical foundation for why working conditions dimensions (autonomy, intensity) matter for worker welfare, and how they interact.

## 5. European Labor Market Institutions and Youth Labor Markets

---

### Jaimovich and Siu (2020) -- Job Polarization and Jobless Recoveries

- **Journal:** Review of Economics and Statistics, 102(1), 129--147
- **Proximity:** 4 (background, structural change in recessions)
- **Main contribution:** Shows that 88% of routine occupation job loss occurs during recessions, explaining "jobless recoveries" through permanent occupation restructuring.
- **Identification strategy:** Time-series analysis of employment by occupation type across business cycles.
- **Key finding:** Employment loss in routine occupations is concentrated in recessions. Jobless recoveries explained entirely by lack of recovery in routine occupations.
- **Data:** US CPS (1967--2015).
- **Relevance:** Macro-level mechanism: recessions permanently alter occupation structure, affecting the types of jobs available to labor market entrants.

---

### Barr and Turner (2015) -- Out of Work and Into School: Labor Market Policies and College Enrollment during the Great Recession

- **Journal:** Journal of Public Economics, 124, 63--73
- **Proximity:** 4 (background, selection into education)
- **Main contribution:** Shows that recessions push workers back into education, with UI policies amplifying the effect.
- **Identification strategy:** Age-based comparison + cross-state variation in UI policies.
- **Key finding:** 10 additional weeks of UI benefits increase enrollment likelihood by 1.8 pp (~20%). Mid-to-late twenties more responsive than 18--19 year olds.
- **Data:** US CPS + UI policy data.
- **Relevance:** Addresses the selection concern: who enters during recessions vs. who delays entry through education. Critical for identification.

## 6. Theoretical Foundations

---

### Topel and Ward (1992) -- Job Mobility and the Careers of Young Men

- **Journal:** Quarterly Journal of Economics, 107(2), 439--479
- **Proximity:** 4 (foundational, early career dynamics)
- **Main contribution:** Establishes key facts about early-career job mobility: workers hold ~7 jobs in first 10 years; wage gains at job changes account for at least 1/3 of early wage growth.
- **Identification strategy:** Descriptive longitudinal analysis.
- **Key finding:** After initial period of weak attachment, careers stabilize. Wage at current job is key determinant of job-changing decisions. Job shopping is essential for career development.
- **Data:** LEED (Longitudinal Employer-Employee Data).
- **Relevance:** Foundational for understanding how early-career job mobility (disrupted by recession entry) shapes long-term outcomes.

---

### Gibbons and Katz (1991) -- Layoffs and Lemons

- **Journal:** Journal of Labor Economics, 9(4), 351--380
- **Proximity:** 4 (theoretical, adverse selection)
- **Main contribution:** Develops and tests asymmetric information model of layoffs: market infers that laid-off workers are low ability ("lemons").
- **Identification strategy:** Comparison of workers displaced by layoffs vs. plant closings (the latter having no adverse selection).
- **Key finding:** Post-displacement wages are lower and unemployment spells longer for layoffs than plant closings, but pre-displacement wages do not differ -- supporting the lemons model.
- **Data:** CPS Displaced Worker Supplements.
- **Relevance:** Theoretical foundation for why recession entry may be different from "normal" entry: adverse selection / signaling may or may not apply to recession entrants.

---

### Ellwood (1982) -- Teenage Unemployment: Permanent Scars or Temporary Blemishes?

- **Journal:** NBER chapter in "The Youth Labor Market Problem" (Freeman and Wise, eds.)
- **Proximity:** 4 (foundational, seminal framing -- CORRECTED from 5; this is a framing paper, not a direct competitor)
- **Main contribution:** Original framing of the scarring question. Investigates whether early unemployment leaves permanent scars or temporary blemishes.
- **Identification strategy:** Longitudinal analysis of youth employment spells.
- **Key finding:** Early unemployment reduces future employment and wages, but effects decay quickly. No evidence of a "vicious cycle" of recurrent unemployment.
- **Data:** NLSY (early waves).
- **Relevance:** Origin of the "scarring vs. blemishes" framing that defines the entire literature. Our paper contributes to the scarring side of this debate for working conditions.

- **Journal:** American Economic Review, 103(5), 1553--1597

## 8. Frontier Map

### 8.1. Key Dimensions Along Which the Literature Varies

| Dimension | Range in Literature |
|-----------|-------------------|
| **Outcome** | Earnings > Employment > Occupation/Skills > Health/Mortality > Working Conditions (sparse) |
| **Geography** | US (dominant) > Canada > Northern Europe (Norway, Germany, Austria, Belgium) > Southern Europe (Spain) > Cross-country |
| **Data type** | Administrative (dominant) > Large survey (CPS/ACS) > Specialized survey (PIAAC, EWCS) |
| **Method** | Cohort x region variation (dominant) > IV (firm size, stock market) > Structural models |
| **Entry measure** | Unemployment rate at graduation (dominant) > GDP growth > Employment rate > Vacancies |
| **Time horizon** | 10--15 years (typical) > 20+ years (some papers) > Full career (rare) |
| **Working conditions measured** | Cognitive skills (Arellano-Bover 2022) > Non-pay amenities via revealed preference (Mahajan et al.) > Direct survey measures (GAP) |

### 8.2. Where Our Paper Sits

Our paper sits at the intersection of:
- **Outcome:** Working conditions (measured directly and multidimensionally via EWCS)
- **Geography:** Pan-European (30+ countries with institutional variation)
- **Data:** EWCS (repeated cross-section, rich working conditions measures)
- **Method:** Cohort x country variation in unemployment rates at entry
- **Entry measure:** Country-specific unemployment rates at entry
- **Time horizon:** Across waves spanning ~25 years of working lives
- **Working conditions measured:** Seven dimensions (autonomy, intensity, physical environment, social environment, skills, working time, prospects)

### 8.3. The Specific Gap We Fill

1. **Direct measurement of working conditions:** Unlike Mahajan et al. (forthcoming), which infers non-pay amenities from revealed preference, we directly measure working conditions using validated EWCS indices (autonomy, intensity, physical risk, social environment, etc.).

2. **Multidimensional working conditions:** Unlike Arellano-Bover (2022), which focuses on cognitive skills via PIAAC, we examine the full set of working conditions dimensions, allowing us to decompose where scarring is concentrated.

3. **European institutional variation:** Unlike most US-focused papers, our pan-European setting allows us to examine how institutions (employment protection, collective bargaining, dual labor markets) mediate the relationship between entry conditions and working conditions.

4. **Working conditions as the primary outcome, not just mechanism:** Most papers treat job quality as a mechanism for earnings scarring. We make working conditions the primary outcome, arguing they are welfare-relevant in their own right.

### 8.4. Closest Three Competitors and How We Differ

| Paper | Overlap | How We Differ |
|-------|---------|--------------|
| **Arellano-Bover (2022, REStat)** | Uses cross-country data; studies entry conditions and long-term skills | We use EWCS (richer working conditions), not PIAAC (skills only). We measure autonomy, intensity, physical/social environment, not just cognitive skills. |
| **Mahajan, Patki, and Stuber (forthcoming, JLE)** | Studies entry conditions and non-pay amenities/working conditions | They use revealed-preference (US admin data); we directly measure conditions (European survey). Our measures are multidimensional and validated. |
| **Cockx and Ghirelli (2016, Lab Econ)** | European rigid labor market; differential channels by education | They study Belgium only, focus on wages/hours. We study working conditions across 30+ countries with institutional variation. |

### 8.5. Contribution Statement (Suggested)

### CATEGORY A: Compensating Differentials and Non-Wage Amenities

This literature provides the theoretical and empirical underpinning for treating working conditions as a key component of total compensation. Essential for framing our paper's contribution: if working conditions are part of an equalizing differences equilibrium, then recession-entry workers who earn less may or may not be compensated with better non-wage amenities.

---

### Rosen (1986) -- The Theory of Equalizing Differences

- **Journal:** Handbook of Labor Economics, Vol. 1, Chapter 12, pp. 641--692 (Ashenfelter and Layard, eds.)
- **Proximity:** 2 (foundational theory directly motivating our research question)
- **Main contribution:** Canonical theoretical treatment of compensating wage differentials. Develops the hedonic model of labor markets in which workers trade off wages against job attributes (risk, effort, scheduling, physical conditions). Establishes that in equilibrium, observed wage differentials reflect both worker preferences for amenities and firm costs of providing them.
- **Identification strategy:** Theoretical (hedonic equilibrium framework).
- **Key finding:** Wage differentials across jobs reflect differences in nonmonetary attributes. The slope of the hedonic wage function identifies neither supply nor demand but a market equilibrium envelope. Empirical applications document compensating differentials for onerous working conditions, health risks, shift work, and inflexible schedules.
- **Data:** Theoretical + review of empirical literature.
- **Relevance:** The foundational theoretical framework for our paper. If entry conditions push workers into jobs with different working conditions, Rosen's framework predicts corresponding wage adjustments. Our paper tests whether the scarring channel operates through the non-wage side of the compensation package, as Rosen's theory would predict.

---

### Sorkin (2018) -- Ranking Firms Using Revealed Preference

- **Journal:** Quarterly Journal of Economics, 133(3), 1331--1393
- **Proximity:** 3 (methodological and conceptual precedent for amenity valuation)
- **Main contribution:** Develops a revealed-preference method to rank firms by desirability using employer-to-employer worker flows. Shows that compensating differentials explain a substantial share of the firm component of earnings variance -- workers systematically move to lower-paying firms when those firms offer better non-wage attributes.
- **Identification strategy:** Structural analysis of employer-to-employer transitions in US administrative data (LEHD), using eigenvector methods from numerical linear algebra to extract a ranking from the flow matrix.
- **Key finding:** Compensating differentials account for over half of the firm component of the variance of earnings. Workers do not simply move to higher-paying firms; they trade off pay for amenities in a way consistent with hedonic equilibrium.
- **Data:** US Longitudinal Employer-Household Dynamics (LEHD), 1985--2012.
- **Relevance:** Establishes that non-wage amenities are quantitatively important in firm-worker sorting. Directly relevant because Mahajan et al. (forthcoming) build on this approach to study recession-entry effects. Our paper complements this by directly measuring the amenities rather than inferring them from worker flows.

---

### Mas and Pallais (2017) -- Valuing Alternative Work Arrangements

- **Journal:** American Economic Review, 107(12), 3722--3759
- **Proximity:** 3 (direct measurement of working conditions valuations)
- **Main contribution:** Uses a large-scale discrete-choice experiment embedded in a real hiring process for a national call center to estimate workers' willingness-to-pay for alternative work arrangements (flexible scheduling, working from home, employer-discretion scheduling).
- **Identification strategy:** Randomized field experiment: during the application process, workers were randomly offered choices between traditional 9-to-5 office positions and alternatives with different attributes at different wages.
- **Key finding:** The average worker is willing to give up 20% of wages to avoid a schedule set by the employer on short notice, and 8% of wages for the option to work from home. Most workers are not willing to pay much for scheduling flexibility, but a tail of high-valuation workers allows for sizable compensating differentials. Women, especially those with young children, have higher WTP for scheduling flexibility.
- **Data:** Field experiment with ~7,000 applicants to a national call center.
- **Relevance:** Provides direct experimental evidence on how workers value specific working conditions dimensions (scheduling flexibility, autonomy, location). Validates the idea that working conditions have measurable welfare value and that focusing only on wages misses important dimensions of compensation.

---

### Maestas, Mullen, Powell, von Wachter, and Wenger (2023) -- The Value of Working Conditions in the United States and Implications for the Structure of Wages

- **Journal:** American Economic Review, 113(7), 2007--2047
- **Proximity:** 2 (directly relevant -- values the same kind of working conditions we measure)
- **Main contribution:** Documents variation in working conditions across US workers and estimates willingness-to-pay for a comprehensive set of working conditions using stated-preference experiments. Shows that accounting for working conditions preferences often exacerbates (rather than equalizes) measured wage inequality.
- **Identification strategy:** Stated-preference discrete choice experiments embedded in the RAND American Life Panel, covering 13 working conditions dimensions.
- **Key finding:** Workers place substantial value on working conditions (physical demands, autonomy, schedule flexibility, job security, etc.). Total compensation inequality is larger than wage inequality because disadvantaged workers both earn less and have worse working conditions -- contradicting the simplest compensating differentials prediction.
- **Data:** RAND American Life Panel + O*NET for working conditions characterization.
- **Relevance:** Critically important for our framing. If recession-entry workers have both lower wages AND worse working conditions, the welfare cost of bad entry timing is even larger than earnings-only estimates suggest. This paper provides the valuation framework to make that argument. Note: the author list is Maestas, Mullen, Powell, von Wachter, and Wenger (not Zamarro as initially suggested by the critic).

---

### Taber and Vejlin (2020) -- Estimation of a Roy/Search/Compensating Differential Model of the Labor Market

- **Journal:** Econometrica, 88(3), 1031--1069
- **Proximity:** 3 (structural model combining compensating differentials with search frictions)
- **Main contribution:** Develops and estimates a structural model integrating Roy-type selection, on-the-job search, and compensating differentials. Decomposes the sources of wage inequality into premarket skills, search frictions, compensating differentials, and on-the-job human capital accumulation.
- **Identification strategy:** Structural estimation using Danish administrative employer-employee matched data, with moment-matching approach to identify model parameters.
- **Key finding:** Premarket skill variation (the Roy component) is the most important source of wage variation. Compensating differentials play a modest but non-trivial role. Search frictions lead to temporary mismatches in amenity allocation.
- **Data:** Danish administrative matched employer-employee data.
- **Relevance:** Provides theoretical framework for how search frictions (which are more binding for recession-entry workers) interact with compensating differentials. In their model, workers in downturns may accept worse matches on both wage and amenity dimensions due to reduced outside options -- consistent with scarring on working conditions.

### CATEGORY B: Econometric Methods for Repeated Cross-Section Cohort Estimation

These papers address the core methodological challenge of our paper: estimating cohort-level effects from repeated cross-sectional data (EWCS).

---

### Deaton (1985) -- Panel Data from Time Series of Cross-Sections

- **Journal:** Journal of Econometrics, 30(1--2), 109--126
- **Proximity:** 2 (core methodological foundation for our empirical approach)
- **Main contribution:** Develops the pseudo-panel methodology for constructing cohort-level panel data from repeated independent cross-sections. Shows how to track "cohorts" (groups with fixed membership, identified as they appear in successive surveys) to estimate models that would otherwise require true panel data.
- **Identification strategy:** Methodological contribution. Proposes using cohort means from successive cross-sections as observations in a panel, with cohort fixed effects replacing individual fixed effects.
- **Key finding:** Under standard assumptions, cohort-level means from repeated cross-sections can be used to estimate dynamic models. Measurement error from finite cohort cell sizes is an errors-in-variables problem that can be addressed with IV or by using sufficiently large cells.
- **Data:** Methodological (illustrated with UK Family Expenditure Survey).
- **Relevance:** Directly applicable to our setting: EWCS is a repeated cross-section, and we observe country-cohort cells across survey waves. Deaton's framework provides the formal justification for our pseudo-panel approach to estimating scarring effects.

---

### Moulton (1990) -- An Illustration of a Pitfall in Estimating the Effects of Aggregate Variables on Micro Units

- **Journal:** Review of Economics and Statistics, 72(2), 334--338
- **Proximity:** 2 (essential methods reference for inference)
- **Main contribution:** Demonstrates that standard errors are severely downward biased when aggregate-level regressors (e.g., state/country unemployment rates) are used in individual-level regressions without accounting for the grouped structure of errors. OLS standard errors can be wrong by orders of magnitude, leading to spurious statistical significance.
- **Identification strategy:** Methodological illustration using wage regressions with state-level regressors estimated on individual-level CPS data.
- **Key finding:** Using geographic aggregate regressors in micro regressions without clustering produces standard errors that are 2--3 times too small, leading researchers to find statistically significant effects where none may exist.
- **Data:** CPS (illustration).
- **Relevance:** Directly relevant to our identification: we regress individual-level working conditions on country-cohort level unemployment rates at entry. We must cluster standard errors at the country-cohort level (or use two-way clustering) to avoid the Moulton problem. This paper must be cited when discussing our inference strategy.

### Eurofound (2022) -- Working Conditions in the Time of COVID-19: Implications for the Future (EWCTS 2021 Overview Report)

- **Journal:** Eurofound Report (Luxembourg: Publications Office of the European Union)
- **Proximity:** 3 (data documentation for the 2021 EWCS wave)
- **Main contribution:** Overview report for the 2021 European Working Conditions Telephone Survey (EWCTS), which covered 70,000+ workers in 36 countries during the COVID-19 pandemic. Documents working conditions during a period of unprecedented disruption, including remote work patterns, work intensity, and health and well-being outcomes.
- **Identification strategy:** Descriptive analysis of survey data.
- **Key finding:** Around 30% of workers were in "strained" jobs (demands exceeding resources). Remote work expanded dramatically. Physical demands remained prevalent (71% reported repetitive hand/arm movements). Working conditions varied sharply by sector, with pandemic-exposed sectors experiencing worse conditions.
- **Data:** EWCTS 2021, 70,000+ workers in 36 countries. Note: this wave used CATI (telephone) rather than face-to-face interviewing, which may affect comparability with earlier waves.
- **Relevance:** Documentation for the most recent EWCS wave. If we include the 2021 data, we must address the mode-switch issue (CATI vs. face-to-face). This report also provides benchmarks for COVID-era working conditions that contextualize the most recent entry cohorts.

### Green, Mostafa, Parent-Thirion, Vermeylen, van Houten, Biletta, and Lyly-Yrjanainen (2013) -- Is Job Quality Becoming More Unequal?

- **Journal:** ILR Review, 66(4), 753--784
- **Proximity:** 3 (directly relevant measurement and trends paper using EWCS)
- **Main contribution:** Examines trends in the level and dispersion of non-wage job quality across 15 EU countries, 1995--2010. Constructs four indices of non-wage job quality: Work Quality (skill use, autonomy, task discretion), Work Intensity, Good Physical Environment, and Working Time Quality.
- **Identification strategy:** Descriptive trend analysis using EWCS waves 1995, 2000, 2005, 2010.
- **Key finding:** Work Quality (skills, autonomy) improved modestly over the period. Work Intensity increased. Physical environment improved. Working time quality changed little. Inequality in job quality (measured by variance) increased in several dimensions, particularly work intensity. Between-country differences narrowed but within-country inequality grew.
- **Data:** EWCS waves 1--5 (1991--2010), 15 EU countries.
- **Relevance:** Uses the same data and constructs we will use. Provides baseline trends in the outcome variables of interest. If job quality inequality is increasing over time, recession-entry cohorts may face compounding disadvantages.

### Clark (2005) -- Your Money or Your Life: Changing Job Quality in OECD Countries

- **Journal:** British Journal of Industrial Relations, 43(3), 377--400
- **Proximity:** 3 (job quality trends, OECD context)
- **Main contribution:** Documents the paradox that despite rising real wages and falling working hours in OECD countries, job satisfaction is stable or declining. Points to work intensification and declining autonomy as likely explanations.
- **Identification strategy:** Descriptive cross-country analysis using ISSP (International Social Survey Programme) and national survey data.
- **Key finding:** Rising real earnings are offset by declining non-wage job quality. Stress and hard work appear to be the primary culprits. Job security also declined. There is increasing inequality across several job quality dimensions.
- **Data:** ISSP 1989 and 1997, plus national surveys.
- **Relevance:** Motivates the argument that non-wage working conditions are an important and distinct welfare dimension. If job quality trends are negative independently of entry conditions, our identification must carefully separate cohort effects from period effects.

---

### Siegrist (1996) -- Adverse Health Effects of High-Effort/Low-Reward Conditions

- **Journal:** Journal of Occupational Health Psychology, 1(1), 27--41
- **Proximity:** 4 (theoretical model of working conditions and health)
- **Main contribution:** Proposes the effort-reward imbalance (ERI) model as a framework for understanding how working conditions affect health. Complements Karasek's (1979) demand-control model by focusing on the reciprocity of exchange: workers who exert high effort but receive low rewards (pay, esteem, job security, promotion prospects) experience chronic stress.
- **Identification strategy:** Conceptual model + prospective cohort study validation on blue-collar workers.
- **Key finding:** High effort combined with low reward (measured by job insecurity, poor promotion prospects, low pay relative to effort) independently predicts new cardiovascular events. The model identifies three reward domains: money, esteem, and status control (job security and career prospects).
- **Data:** Prospective cohort of blue-collar men (for validation).
- **Relevance:** Provides the theoretical link between working conditions and health outcomes that complements Karasek (1979). Together, these two models (demand-control and effort-reward imbalance) justify the EWCS working conditions dimensions as welfare-relevant: recession-entry workers may face high demands with low control (Karasek) and high effort with low reward (Siegrist), both channels for health damage.

### CATEGORY D: Additional European Scarring Evidence

### Raaum and Roed (2006) -- Do Business Cycle Conditions at the Time of Labor Market Entry Affect Future Employment Prospects?

- **Journal:** Review of Economics and Statistics, 88(2), 193--210
- **Proximity:** 3 (closely related, Norwegian evidence on employment scarring)
- **Main contribution:** Studies whether labor market conditions at the time of leaving secondary education persistently affect employment prospects in Norway. Finds substantial and long-lasting effects, particularly for those entering during downturns.
- **Identification strategy:** Cohort variation in local unemployment rates at the time of completing secondary education.
- **Key finding:** Entering the labor market during a downturn significantly increases the probability of non-employment during prime working years. Effects persist for at least 10 years. The scarring mechanism operates primarily through reduced employment probability rather than wage effects.
- **Data:** Norwegian administrative register data.
- **Relevance:** Key Scandinavian evidence that scarring operates through employment channels rather than wages in strong welfare-state settings. Relevant to our cross-country institutional analysis: in countries with strong safety nets, scarring may appear in working conditions and employment rather than wages.

### Forsythe (2022) -- Why Don't Firms Hire Young Workers During Recessions?

- **Journal:** Economic Journal, 132(645), 1765--1789
- **Proximity:** 3 (mechanism paper on why entry conditions disproportionately affect youth)
- **Main contribution:** Provides evidence that the hiring probability of young workers is more cyclically sensitive than for experienced workers. Uses detailed analysis of worker flows by experience level to show that shifts in labor demand are the primary driver of cyclical youth employment declines.
- **Identification strategy:** Cross-state variation in unemployment rates combined with CPS worker-flow data, decomposing employment changes into hiring, separation, and quit rates by experience level.
- **Key finding:** Youth hiring rates are substantially more procyclical than those of experienced workers. The gap is driven primarily by reduced job creation for entry-level positions during recessions, not by differential separation rates. This suggests demand-side mechanisms dominate.
- **Data:** US CPS (matched monthly panels), state-level variation.
- **Relevance:** Provides the demand-side mechanism for why entry conditions matter: firms reduce entry-level hiring more than experienced hiring during downturns, forcing young workers into worse initial matches. This links to our working conditions outcomes if the reduced set of available entry-level jobs during recessions systematically offers worse conditions.

### Cutler, Huang, and Lleras-Muney (2016) -- Economic Conditions and Mortality: Evidence from 200 Years of Data

- **Journal:** NBER Working Paper 22690
- **Proximity:** 3 (health scarring from economic conditions across the life cycle)
- **Main contribution:** Uses data spanning 200 years across 32 countries to examine how economic conditions at different life stages affect later mortality. Finds that economic booms during adolescence lower adult mortality, while adverse conditions at any point in early life increase later mortality.
- **Identification strategy:** Cross-cohort variation in GDP per capita at different ages, using 100+ birth cohorts across 32 countries.
- **Key finding:** Booms during ages 0--25, particularly adolescence, reduce adult mortality. Adverse conditions during fetal development and childhood also increase later mortality. Air pollution and alcohol consumption increase during booms (a contemporaneous health cost), while adolescent booms raise adult incomes and improve mental health (a long-run benefit).
- **Data:** Human Mortality Database + Maddison GDP data, 32 countries, 200 years.
- **Relevance:** Provides the cross-country, long-run context for health scarring from economic conditions. Confirms that early-life economic conditions have persistent effects through health channels, complementing the working conditions pathway we study. Note: this was cited as "Cutler et al. 2016" in the first-pass frontier map; now correctly identified with full author list.

### Barrero, Bloom, and Davis (2023) -- The Evolution of Work from Home

- **Journal:** Journal of Economic Perspectives, 37(4), 23--50
- **Proximity:** 4 (background on post-COVID working conditions transformation)
- **Main contribution:** Documents the dramatic and persistent shift to working from home (WFH) following the COVID-19 pandemic. Shows WFH stabilized at about 25--30% of paid workdays, compared to 5% pre-pandemic.
- **Identification strategy:** Descriptive analysis using the Survey of Working Arrangements and Attitudes (SWAA), monthly surveys from May 2020 onward.
- **Key finding:** WFH has stabilized at roughly 25--30% of paid workdays (5x the pre-pandemic level). Employers and employees both see benefits. The shift is driven by better-than-expected WFH experiences, sunk investments in remote work infrastructure, and reduced stigma. Workers value WFH at about 8% of pay on average.
- **Data:** Survey of Working Arrangements and Attitudes (SWAA), 2020--2023.
- **Relevance:** Contextualizes the post-COVID working conditions landscape. For cohorts entering the labor market during/after COVID, the set of available working conditions (remote work, autonomy, scheduling flexibility) differs fundamentally from pre-COVID norms. Our paper should acknowledge this structural break when interpreting results for the most recent entry cohorts using the 2021 EWCTS wave.
