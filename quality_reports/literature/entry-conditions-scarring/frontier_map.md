### Dimension 3: Data Type

| Data | Strengths | Weakness |
|------|-----------|----------|
| Administrative (dominant) | Large N, long panels, precise earnings | No working conditions measures |

### Dimension 4: Mechanisms Explored

| Mechanism | Evidence | Our Angle |
|-----------|----------|-----------|
| Firm quality / sorting | Strong (Oreopoulos et al. 2012, Arellano-Bover 2024) | We observe working conditions at the firm level |
| Skill mismatch | Moderate (Liu et al. 2016, Altonji et al. 2016) | We measure skills & discretion index directly |
| Human capital / on-the-job learning | Growing (Arellano-Bover & Saltiel 2024) | We measure skill development and training access |
| Occupation downgrading | Moderate (Kahn 2010, Huckfeldt 2022) | We observe occupation-level working conditions |
| Institutional mediation | Limited (Genda et al. 2010, Cockx & Ghirelli 2016) | We exploit 30+ countries with different institutions |
| Compensating differentials | Growing (Sorkin 2018, Maestas et al. 2023, Taber & Vejlin 2020) | We test whether recession entrants get worse conditions AND lower pay |

### Dimension 5: Compensating Differentials Framework (NEW -- second pass)

| Paper | Approach | Finding |
|-------|----------|---------|
| Rosen (1986) | Theoretical hedonic model | Workers trade wages for amenities in equilibrium |
| Sorkin (2018, QJE) | Revealed preference from worker flows | Compensating differentials explain >50% of firm wage variance |
| Mas & Pallais (2017, AER) | Field experiment | Workers pay 8--20% of wages for scheduling flexibility/WFH |
| Maestas et al. (2023, AER) | Stated preference experiments | Disadvantaged workers have BOTH lower pay and worse conditions |
| Taber & Vejlin (2020, Econometrica) | Structural Roy/search/CD model | Premarket skills dominate, but search frictions distort amenity allocation |
| Mahajan et al. (forthcoming, JLE) | Revealed preference applied to entry conditions | 25% of recession-entry earnings loss offset by amenities |

## 3. What Has Been Done vs. What Remains

### Done:
- Earnings scarring is thoroughly documented in US, Canada, and several European countries
- Employment and hours effects are well understood
- Skill mismatch and occupation downgrading mechanisms are partially documented
- Firm quality as a mechanism is established (Arellano-Bover 2024)
- Health and mortality consequences are emerging (Schwandt & von Wachter 2020 WP)
- Compensating differentials for working conditions are established theoretically (Rosen 1986) and empirically (Maestas et al. 2023, Sorkin 2018)

### Partially Done:
- Cross-country comparison with institutional variation (only Genda et al. 2010 for JP-US comparison)
- Non-pay amenities as outcome (only Mahajan et al. via revealed preference)
- Cognitive skills as outcome (only Arellano-Bover 2022 via PIAAC)
- Whether recession-entry workers face worse conditions AND lower pay (Maestas et al. 2023 show this cross-sectionally but not for entry conditions)

### Not Done (Our Contribution):
- Multidimensional working conditions (autonomy, intensity, physical environment, social environment, skills & discretion, working time, prospects) as primary outcome
- Pan-European analysis exploiting institutional heterogeneity across 30+ countries
- Decomposition of which working conditions dimensions are most affected by entry conditions
- Interaction between entry conditions and labor market institutions on working conditions
- Direct welfare implications beyond earnings (working conditions matter independently)
